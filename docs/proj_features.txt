I think you're describing something bigger than a productivity app. You're describing a Personal Life Operating System (LifeOS) that understands behavior from multiple signals, not just tasks.

The key is to make it privacy-first and optional. Users should choose which signals to share.

1. Context Engine
Instead of just knowing your tasks, the AI knows your context.

Examples:

📍 Home

🏢 Office

📚 Library

☕ Coffee shop

🏋️ Gym

🛏 Bedroom

🍽 Dining area

The user can define these places once.

Then the AI adapts:

At Library

"This is usually your highest-focus location. Shall we start the hardest task?"

At Home (late evening)

"Let's wrap up with a 15-minute review instead of starting something new."

2. Movement Intelligence
Instead of just counting steps:

Daily steps

Sitting duration

Walking breaks

Commute time

Standing time

Example:

"You've been sitting for 2.5 hours. A 5-minute walk now could help before your next focus session."

3. Breathing & Stress Detection ⭐⭐⭐
Breathing is interesting because it can reflect stress or relaxation, but it's important to be realistic about what consumer devices can measure.

If the user has a smartwatch or compatible wearable, the app could use available data such as:

Heart rate

Heart rate variability (HRV)

Respiratory rate (if the device provides it)

Blood oxygen (SpO₂), where available

Without a wearable, the app can offer guided breathing exercises or estimate breathing during a phone-based breathing session, but it shouldn't claim continuous breathing measurement.

Possible interpretation:

Elevated heart rate + low HRV + task avoidance
→ User may be stressed.

AI response:

"You seem tense. Let's do a one-minute breathing exercise before deciding whether to continue."

One caution
For a hackathon, don't try to build every sensor integration. Instead:

Integrate with health data available through platforms like Google Health Connect (Android) if the user grants permission.

Use phone and calendar signals that are easy to access.

Simulate advanced reasoning using the data you do have.

The innovation isn't that you measured breathing or steps—many apps already do that. The innovation is that your AI combines those signals with psychology, behavior, and task management to answer:

"Given your current energy, stress, location, and history, what's the best next action for you right now?"

That's a much stronger and more distinctive value proposition than simply collecting more health data.
